Penguins

Masters of the Antarctic and Ocean

About Penguins

Penguins are flightless seabirds that live almost exclusively in the Southern Hemisphere. These remarkable birds have evolved to be expert swimmers and divers, spending much of their lives in the ocean hunting for fish, squid, and krill.


Despite being unable to fly, penguins are incredibly agile in water, using their flipper-like wings to "fly" through the ocean at speeds up to 22 mph (35 km/h).

Habitat & Distribution

While most people associate penguins with Antarctica, they actually live throughout the Southern Hemisphere. You can find penguins in Antarctica, South America, Africa, Australia, and New Zealand.


They prefer cold ocean currents and often live on islands and coastlines where they can easily access the sea for hunting while having safe places on land for breeding and raising their young.

Popular Penguin Species

Emperor Penguin
Stable

Height: Up to 122 cm (48 in)
Weight: Up to 45 kg (99 lbs)
Location: Antarctica
The largest penguin species, famous for their incredible breeding journey across Antarctic ice.

King Penguin
Stable

Height: Up to 100 cm (39 in)
Weight: Up to 18 kg (40 lbs)
Location: Subantarctic islands
Distinguished by their bright orange neck patches and silver-grey backs.

Adelie Penguin
Stable

Height: Up to 70 cm (28 in)
Weight: Up to 6 kg (13 lbs)
Location: Antarctica
Classic black and white appearance with distinctive white eye rings.

Humboldt Penguin
Vulnerable

Height: Up to 70 cm (28 in)
Weight: Up to 5 kg (11 lbs)
Location: Peru & Chile
Lives in warmer climates along the South American coast.

African Penguin
Endangered

Height: Up to 70 cm (28 in)
Weight: Up to 5 kg (11 lbs)
Location: South Africa
Also known as the "Jackass Penguin" due to their donkey-like call.

Little Blue Penguin
Stable

Height: Up to 33 cm (13 in)
Weight: Up to 1.2 kg (2.6 lbs)
Location: Australia & New Zealand
The smallest penguin species, also called Fairy Penguins.

🎉 Amazing Penguin Facts

  • Penguin huddles: Emperor Penguins form huddles of up to 10,000 individuals to stay warm in -40°C weather!
  • Monogamous mates: Many penguin species mate for life and can recognize their partner's call among thousands of other penguins.
  • Pebble gifts: Adelie Penguins give pebbles as gifts to their mates as part of their courtship ritual.
  • Swimming speed: Gentoo Penguins are the fastest swimmers, reaching speeds of up to 36 km/h (22 mph)!
  • Waterproof feathers: Penguins have about 100 feathers per square inch, creating a waterproof barrier.
  • Counter-current heat exchange: Their blood vessels are arranged to minimize heat loss in cold water.
  • Excellent vision: Penguins can see clearly both above and below water, with special adaptations for underwater hunting.
  • Social creatures: Penguins live in colonies called rookeries that can contain hundreds of thousands of individuals.
